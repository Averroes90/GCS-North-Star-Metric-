The problem in one paragraph

PANW's Global Customer Services org is shifting from a Total Contract Value (TCV) sales model, where reps are credited on the full multi-year dollar value at signing, to an ARR + consumption hybrid, where revenue arrives only if customers actively use what they bought. This breaks the old incentive system: a rep can close a massive TCV deal and walk away, but in the new world, a deal that never gets deployed or consumed is a future churn event. Leadership needs a North Star metric that fairly measures success across four things at once, booking the contract, deploying the product, keeping it technically healthy, and sustaining usage over the customer's lifecycle.

What this document does

It is a working reference for designing that metric. It (1) defines the four dimensions the North Star must balance, (2) enumerates every major existing metric in the SaaS/CS landscape and scores how well each covers those dimensions, (3) explains the underlying concepts you need to defend design choices in Q&A, (4) lays out the five messy-data edge cases the brief calls out, and (5) distills the case for a composite index.

How to use it

Read Four Dimensions first, everything else depends on those definitions. Then open the Metrics Explorer and sort/filter to pressure-test any metric you're considering. Key Concepts is the "why it's true" layer for anything the interview panel might challenge. Edge Cases are the specific traps your metric design must handle.

The four dimensions the North Star must balance

These come directly from the case brief: "balances initial contract bookings, full deployment of the products purchased, technically healthy and sustained platform usage over a customer's lifecycle."

1. Initial Contract Bookings

The dollars committed
Definition
The commercial commitment the customer signs, the size of the handshake. Historically captured as TCV; in the new model, a blend of ARR + consumption commit (annual committed credits/dollars).
Why it matters
Bookings fund the business and set the ceiling for everything downstream. You can't consume what wasn't purchased. This dimension keeps the revenue engine accountable.
"Good" looks like
Multi-year commitment right-sized to the customer's actual capacity plan, not oversold, not under-provisioned.
Pitfall
Rewarding this alone (the old TCV world) incentivizes over-selling and shelfware.

2. Full Deployment

The product is stood up
Definition
The degree to which the customer has actually provisioned licenses, configured the product, integrated it into their environment, and operationalized it across the intended user base.
Why it matters
Undeployed product cannot produce value, cannot generate consumption, and almost never renews. Deployment is the bridge from sale to usage.
"Good" looks like
Licenses provisioned, integrations live, key feature milestones hit within a reasonable window (e.g., 60–90 days), broad reach across the buyer's intended team.
Pitfall
"Logo logged in once" gets mistaken for deployment. License provisioning is necessary but not sufficient.

3. Technical Health

The product works well
Definition
The stability and quality of the customer's experience, support burden, severity distribution of tickets, SLA attainment, active escalations.
Why it matters
A heavily-consumed, fully-deployed product can still be a churn risk if it's causing operational pain. Tech health is the hygiene layer, easy to ignore when green, devastating when red.
"Good" looks like
Low Sev-1/Sev-2 rate, SLAs met, zero active exec escalations, declining ticket trend as deployment matures.
Pitfall
Ticket volume alone is ambiguous, heavy users open more tickets. Must weight by severity and look at trajectory.

4. Sustained Platform Usage

The customer keeps using it
Definition
Ongoing, durable consumption and engagement across the customer's lifecycle, not just at onboarding. Captures both level (is usage high enough?) and trend (is it growing or decaying?).
Why it matters
In a consumption-hybrid model, revenue arrives only if usage flows. This is the strongest leading indicator of renewal and expansion.
"Good" looks like
Consumption trending flat or up, stickiness (DAU/MAU) strong, feature depth increasing, usage spread across users and workloads rather than concentrated.
Pitfall
Raw utilization snapshots miss trajectory. An account burning 95% of credits might be spike-and-drop masquerading as healthy.

Key concepts you'll need to defend in Q&A

These are the conceptual frames that sit underneath every decision in the metric design. Panel Q&A will test whether you actually understand the distinctions.

1 · Three ways to size a sale

TCV vs. ARR vs. Consumption Revenue

Every SaaS company is some mix of these three. The shift PANW is making is from a TCV-weighted model to an ARR + Consumption-weighted hybrid.

ConceptWhat it measuresTime horizonBehavior it incentivizes
TCVFull dollar value of contract over entire term, booked at signing.Entire contract (often 3 yrs)Land big upfront commits.
ARRAnnualized value of the recurring subscription, measured at a point in time.Rolling, year-by-yearKeep the subscription alive.
Consumption RevenueRevenue recognized as the customer burns credits/usage.Real-time / monthlyDrive actual product use, not just signatures.

Why it matters: Each metric optimizes for a different moment in the customer journey. The old TCV world ignored everything after signing. The new world can't ignore any of it.

2 · Leading vs. Lagging indicators

Why you need both

A lagging indicator measures something that has already happened (NRR, churn, ARR). A leading indicator predicts what's about to happen (usage trend, Sev-1 rate, DAU/MAU).

A metric built only from lagging signals tells you about last year's decisions, too late to intervene. A metric built only from leading signals has no ground truth. A good North Star blends both: leading signals to drive action, lagging signals to validate that the leading signals were right.

Rule of thumb: comp plans tied to lagging metrics print last year's paycheck for this year's work. Comp plans tied to leading metrics are faster but more gameable. Mix them intentionally.

3 · Level vs. Trend (the derivative matters)

Current utilization vs. utilization trajectory

An account at 60% utilization trending up is healthier than one at 85% trending down. Most existing health metrics report only the level (snapshot). Including the trend (first derivative) is where a thoughtful composite wins.

The specific signal to capture: consumption rate over the last 30–90 days relative to the 12-month baseline. Spike-and-drop patterns show a negative derivative even when the level still looks fine.

4 · Composite indexes, the pattern & its failure modes

Weighted blends: what they buy you, what they cost

A composite index combines multiple sub-metrics into one weighted score (typically 0–100). This is the industry-standard pattern for customer health (Gainsight, ChurnZero, Totango).

Strengths: defensible coverage of multiple dimensions, surface-level simplicity for executives, component-level diagnostics for CSMs, tunable as the business evolves.

Failure modes to address in the design:

  • Arbitrary weights. Why 40/20/20/20 and not 30/30/20/20? Anchor weights to observed renewal correlation, not opinion.
  • Offsetting components. A high Deployment score can mask a collapsing Usage score if you just average. Consider floor rules or multiplicative terms for critical dimensions.
  • Hidden nonlinearity. Going from 90% → 95% utilization isn't the same as 40% → 45%. Use thresholds, piecewise functions, or logistic transforms where linear averaging hides real dynamics.
  • Weight drift. As the product/business evolves, so should the weights. Build in a re-calibration cadence.
5 · Gameability (for comp plans)

Can a rep manipulate this metric without actually creating value?

When a metric drives compensation, its gameability becomes a first-class design concern. A few patterns to watch for:

  • Utilization: rep can push customer to burn credits unnecessarily.
  • NPS/CSAT: timing the survey after a "good moment" skews results.
  • Ticket volume: CSMs can discourage ticket filing.
  • "Color" status: manual rating is prone to sandbagging just before reviews.

Composites with technical-health + trend components tend to be the hardest to game, because manipulating one lever (say, usage) damages another (say, support load spikes).

6 · Metric hierarchy

North Star ≠ only metric

A good North Star is a single number at the top, backed by a small set of input metrics one level down, backed by diagnostic metrics at the operational layer. The North Star is for the VP's dashboard; the inputs are for CSM action; the diagnostics are for root cause. Don't conflate the three.

The five edge cases the brief calls out

These are not academic, they're the concrete data pathologies your metric must handle. Expect every Q&A question to come back to one of these. The metric will be evaluated on how it treats each.

Spike & Drop ~5% of accounts

Pattern: Burns 90% of annual credits in Month 1, then near-zero.

What naive metrics miss: Annual utilization looks 100%, "fully realized." Raw burn-down looks healthy.

What the North Star must capture: Negative trend derivative over the last 6–9 months. A momentum/trend component penalizes this even when level looks fine.

Shelfware ~10% of accounts

Pattern: Paid high annual_commit_dollars, but Daily_Usage_Logs are empty.

What naive metrics miss: ARR looks great. TCV looks great. Everything commercial looks great.

What the North Star must capture: Deployment + Usage components collapse. A floor rule on Usage should drop the composite score sharply, regardless of Bookings strength.

Consistent Overages ~15% of accounts

Pattern: Consistently consume 120%+ of included monthly compute credits.

What naive metrics miss: Utilization > 100% looks "broken" to a naive cap. Burn rate alarms fire.

What the North Star must capture: This is a positive signal (product stickiness, expansion candidate), not a penalty. Metric must distinguish "over-consumption against commit" from "spike-and-drop", both involve high burn, opposite meanings.

Mid-Year Expansions

Pattern: Second, larger contract signed mid-year, overlapping the first.

What naive metrics miss: Which contract do you attribute usage to? Do you double-count commit dollars? What start date does the "lifecycle" use?

What the North Star must capture: Metric logic must define contract precedence (e.g., take the most recent, or sum active commits in the overlap window). Document this explicitly in the spec.

Orphaned / Rogue Usage

Pattern: account_id in usage logs doesn't exist in Accounts, or usage falls outside active contract dates.

What naive metrics miss: Silently inflates usage. Corrupts CSM-level rollups.

What the North Star must capture: Pipeline must flag and quarantine these records, never silently drop them. Data quality test is mandatory; the metric itself should expose a "quarantine volume" diagnostic.

AVRI, Account Value Realization Index

Working draft of the proposed North Star metric (mirrors /specs/metric_v0.md). A 0–100 composite at the account level, rolled up to CSM and region. The four pillars map 1:1 to the brief's four dimensions.

In one sentence

AVRI measures whether a customer is realizing the value of what they purchased, combining commit-to-consumption fit, usage momentum, deployment maturity, and technical health.

The four pillars

Commit Realization (CR)

weight 30%
Are they using what they paid for?
Inputs
Contracts.included_monthly_compute_credits, Daily_Usage_Logs.compute_credits_consumed
Calculation
Trailing-90-day consumed credits ÷ (3 × monthly commit)
Score curve
Piecewise: 0–50% → linear; 50–110% → score = 100 (sweet spot); 110–150% → still 100 (flagged as expansion); 150%+ → gentle decay to ~60.
Why piecewise
Linear utilization punishes overages as if they were equivalent to underuse. They are not, overages are positive churn signals.

Usage Momentum (UM)

weight 30%
Is consumption holding up over time?
Inputs
Daily_Usage_Logs.compute_credits_consumed
Calculation
Ratio of last-90-day daily average to trailing-12-month daily average.
Score curve
Ratio ≥ 1.0 → 100; 0.7–1.0 → 70–100; 0.3–0.7 → 30–70; <0.3 → severe decay zone.
Why this matters
The differentiator vs Gainsight CHS, which is snapshot-only. This is what catches Spike-and-Drop.

Deployment Maturity (DM)

weight 20%
Is the product genuinely operationalized?
Inputs
Daily_Usage_Logs (count of distinct active days)
Calculation
active_days_last_90 ÷ 90.
Score curve
Linear, 0 → 0, 1.0 → 100.
Synthetic-data caveat
In production this pillar would also pull license provisioning %, feature adoption breadth, onboarding milestones. The synthetic schema doesn't include these, active-day breadth is the available proxy.

Technical Health (TH)

weight 20%
Is the platform working for them?
Inputs
Account_Health.health_color, Account_Health.date
Calculation
Exponentially weighted health color over last 90 days. green=100, yellow=50, red=0. Decay factor 0.95.
Score curve
Direct (already 0–100).
Synthetic-data caveat
Production version replaces the color with Sev-1 frequency, SLA attainment, and active escalations.
Composite formula
AVRI_raw = 0.30·CR + 0.30·UM + 0.20·DM + 0.20·TH

# Floor rule: technical health cannot be averaged away
if TH < 30:
    AVRI = min(AVRI_raw, 50)
else:
    AVRI = AVRI_raw

Why the floor rule: linear weighted averages are substitutable, high CR can mathematically offset low TH. In reality, a customer with critical platform issues will not renew regardless of consumption. The floor expresses non-substitutability. trigger · cap

RAG thresholds
ColorRangeInterpretation
GreenAVRI ≥ 75 Healthy. Renewal trajectory positive. Eligible for expansion.
Yellow50 ≤ AVRI < 75 Watch. Specific pillar(s) underperforming.
RedAVRI < 50 At risk. Active renewal risk. Escalate.

v0 caveat: thresholds are heuristic. Production would calibrate against historical renewal outcomes.

Edge case behavior
Edge casePillar responseNet AVRI
Spike & Drop (5%)CR high (annualized), UM near 0, DM lowYellow / Red, caught by UM
Shelfware (10%)CR ≈ 0, UM ≈ 0, DM ≈ 0Deep Red, three pillars collapse
Consistent Overage (15%)CR = 100, UM stable, DM highGreen, correctly rewarded; capacity flag fires separately

Orphaned UsageQuarantined upstream by pipelineNot counted; surfaced via DQ test
Aggregation rules

Account-level: AVRI computed from raw signals as above.
CSM-level: Dollar-weighted average of account AVRIs in the CSM's book, weighted by annual_commit_dollars.
Region-level: Same dollar-weighted rollup across all CSMs in the region.

Why dollar-weighted: a CSM's portfolio health should not be dominated by long-tail small accounts.

v0 explicit non-goals

No predictive ML model, AVRI is a transparent rules-based scorecard (explainability matters for comp plans). No per-segment weight tuning. No NPS/sentiment input (not in schema). No cold-start handling for new accounts (<90 days tenure).

Open design questions

(1) Are the 30/30/20/20 weights defensible without renewal-correlation calibration? (2) Is 90-day the right momentum window? (3) Should weights fork by Enterprise vs Mid-Market? (4) Which AVRI components should drive variable comp? (5) Should the 110–150% utilization band trigger a separate "Capacity Risk" alert independent of AVRI? (6) How do we handle cold-start (accounts <90 days tenure)?

Goals

The dataset must be schema-faithful (5 tables at brief's row counts), realistic enough for existing metrics to compute meaningfully, pathological enough for the 5 edge cases to be visible, and internally consistent across tables.

Reproducibility: single seed (SEED = 20260422) governs all RNG. Re-running produces identical data.

Account personas (population mix)

Every account is assigned exactly one persona. Persona governs the time-series shape of consumption.

Healthy Steady 40%

Ramps over 30–60 days, plateaus at 70–95% of monthly commit, holds steady.

Healthy Growing 15%

Starts at 40%, grows to ~90% by Month 12.

Healthy Mature 10%

High consumption (~85%) from Day 1, no ramp.

Healthy Declining 5%

Starts at 90%, gradually declines to ~55%. Acceptable lifecycle decay (NOT spike-and-drop).

Spike & Drop 5%

Burns 80–95% of annual credits in Month 1, drops to <5% of monthly commit thereafter.

Shelfware 10%

Near-zero usage throughout. Health color drifts toward yellow/red.

Consistent Overage 15%

Consumption holds at 110–150% of monthly commit every month.

Mid-year Expansion overlay (~5%): Independent overlay applied to ~50 accounts (any persona except Shelfware). A second, larger contract is signed 4–8 months in, and consumption ramps to a higher absolute level. Tests the contract-precedence logic.

Time-series shape
consumption(t) = baseline(t, persona)
               × seasonal(t)
               × noise(t)
               × edge_modifier(t, persona)

baseline, deterministic shape per persona.
seasonal, weekdays 1.0, weekends 0.6 ± 0.05.
noise, Gaussian σ=0.15, clipped to [0.5, 1.5].
edge_modifier, persona-specific perturbations (spike-and-drop's Month 1 burst lives here).

Daily totals are split into 1–8 events with a Dirichlet split. Inactive days produce zero logs.

Cross-table correlations

Segment ↔ commit size (Enterprise ~5–10× Mid-Market). Industry ↔ seasonality (Retail Q4 spike, Education summer dip). Persona ↔ health color (Shelfware drifts red; Spike-and-Drop decays through yellow). Persona ↔ active days (Healthy Steady 4–5/week; Shelfware 0–2/quarter). Account-rep segment matching is strict.

Validation gates

Pre-upload assertions: (1) row counts within ±5%; (2) contracts FK integrity holds; (3) ~150 orphan logs detected; (4) ~150 out-of-window logs detected; (5) persona distribution within ±2pp; (6) mid-year expansion accounts have exactly 2 overlapping contracts; (7) account_health is unique by (account_id, week).

Key architectural decisions

1 · Three-tier separation

Generation, transformation, and presentation are decoupled. The Python data generator can be replaced with a real production data feed without touching the SQL pipeline. The SQL pipeline can be edited without touching the dashboard. The dashboard can be redesigned without re-running the pipeline. This is the standard analytics-engineering pattern (often called "extract-load-transform" or ELT) and it's what scales.

2 · Local Parquet first, BigQuery second

The data generator writes Parquet files locally before they're uploaded to BigQuery. This means we can iterate on data shape without paying BigQuery query costs every time, and we have a portable artifact that anyone can inspect with pandas. The upload script is a separate, single-purpose tool, failure modes don't compound.

3 · SQL as the canonical metric definition

The metric formulas live in SQL files, not Python or in the dashboard. This means the metric is portable, you could swap Streamlit for Looker, Tableau, or a Slack bot tomorrow, and the SQL is what they'd all read. SQL is also what data engineers and analytics PMs will need to read and modify in production. The brief explicitly grades on "dbt-style SQL," which is exactly this pattern.

4 · Templated SQL with parameterized as-of-date

Every SQL file accepts a snapshot date as a parameter. Re-running the pipeline with --as-of-date 2025-12-31 produces a fresh snapshot for that date, which is how production systems implement time-series metrics without rewriting queries.

5 · Full reproducibility from one seed

Every random number in the data generator is sourced from SEED=20260422. Re-running on any machine produces byte-identical output. This is critical for the case study, anyone reviewing the repo can git clone, run the scripts, and produce the same dashboard the panelists saw.

6 · Authentication via ADC, not embedded credentials

Every script that touches BigQuery reads credentials from ~/.config/gcloud/application_default_credentials.json, written once by gcloud auth application-default login. No service account key files in the repo, no env vars to set. Standard pattern for any code that talks to GCP from a developer machine.

What to walk away with

If you internalize three things from this document, these are they.

1No single existing metric covers all four dimensions. The best standalone metrics (Utilization, NRR) cover at most two. Every raw metric has a blind spot that one of the five edge cases can exploit. This is the argument for a purpose-built composite, and the structure of this document is the proof.

2Commercial metrics are uniformly lagging. TCV, ARR, NRR, GRR, every dollar-denominated metric tells you what already happened. The leading signal lives in consumption, adoption, and technical-health metrics. A comp plan built only on lagging dimensions pays people for last year's work. The North Star must embed leading signals.

3Existing composites (Gainsight & peers) have known gaps. They're the right pattern but they don't natively handle commit-vs-consume, consistent overages, spike-and-drop trajectory, or orphaned usage. Those gaps are exactly the whitespace your proposal fills. The opportunity isn't "invent a new health score", it's "build the health score PANW's business model actually needs."
